\begin{abstract}
System Neural Diversity (SND) quantifies behavioral heterogeneity in
multi-agent reinforcement learning by averaging pairwise Wasserstein distances
over all $\binom{n}{2}$ agent pairs, scaling as $O(n^2)$; sparse-graph
generalizations are identified as an open problem by the original authors.
We introduce Graph-SND, a graph-structured generalization that computes
pairwise distances only on the edges of a user-specified graph $G$ and
aggregates via a normalized weighted mean. The construction recovers SND
exactly when $G$ is the complete graph and admits two interpretations
determined by the choice of $G$. When $G$ is fixed and encodes task-relevant
interaction structure, Graph-SND is a localized diversity measure, reducing
distance evaluations from $O(n^2)$ to $O(|E|)$. When edges are drawn
independently with Horvitz--Thompson weights, Graph-SND is an unbiased
estimator of full SND, with a Hoeffding-type concentration bound yielding
$O(1/\sqrt{m})$ error in the number of sampled edges $m$, independent of $n$.
We prove six formal properties, recovery, non-negativity,
graph-automorphism invariance, complexity reduction, unbiasedness, and
concentration, and verify all experimentally on VMAS Multi-Agent Goal Navigation at team sizes $n \in \{4, 8, 16, 100\}$. Recovery is numerically exact, the estimator is unbiased to within expected sampling noise, concentration bounds hold with zero violations across 24{,}000 draws, and wall-clock speedup tracks the analytical $\binom{n}{2}/|E|$ ratio, sustaining a ${\sim}\!10\times$ speedup during active PPO training at $n=100$, with the sampling estimator tracking full $\SND$ indistinguishably throughout. In DiCo at $n=16$, replacing the aggregator with Graph-SND matches mean reward and applied-diversity set-point tracking while reducing per-batch subroutine time.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Behavioral diversity is increasingly recognized as a first-class
property of multi-agent reinforcement learning systems, not merely a
by-product of heterogeneous parameterization. Heterogeneous policies
can specialize into complementary roles, remain robust under
observation noise and dynamic disturbances, and expose latent
resilience skills that are invisible in reward curves alone
\citep{bettini2023hetgppo, bettini2025snd}. System Neural Diversity
(SND), introduced by \citet{bettini2025snd}, is the current
statistical metric of this heterogeneity for continuous-action
stochastic policies. SND defines a pairwise Wasserstein distance
between agents' action distributions, averages over a Monte Carlo
rollout set, and aggregates into a global score by taking the mean of
pairwise distances over all unique agent pairs. The metric is
closed-form for Gaussian policies, satisfies the axioms of a
mathematical metric, and enables explicit control of diversity during
training via the DiCo paradigm \citep{bettini2024dico}.

We extend SND along a specific axis that its authors explicitly flag
as open.
\citet[\S 6]{bettini2025snd}
note that SND scales quadratically in the number of agents: evaluating
it requires computing pairwise Wasserstein distances for every one of
the $\binom{n}{2}$ agent pairs at every rollout batch on which
diversity is to be measured or controlled. They further observe that
in large multi-agent systems only locally interacting agents are
typically relevant, and they suggest computing SND only within
subgroups; they then identify \emph{aggregating subgroup values into a
global diversity value} as an open problem. To our knowledge, no
subsequent work has resolved this question.

This paper addresses the gap directly. We define Graph-SND, a
graph-structured generalization that computes pairwise behavioral
distances only on the edges of a user-specified graph $G$ over agents,
and aggregates those distances via a normalized weighted mean. The
construction is minimal by design: it extends rather than replaces
SND, recovers SND exactly when $G$ is complete, and admits two clean
interpretations distinguished entirely by the choice of $G$.

When $G$ is fixed and encodes task-relevant interaction structure
(e.g., the communication graph, $k$-nearest neighbors in observation
space, or a user-provided prior), Graph-SND is a \emph{localized
diversity measure}: it quantifies the heterogeneity among pairs of
agents that actually interact, and ignores pairs that do not. This
choice makes the metric cheaper to compute and aligns its semantics
with the coordination structure of the task. It is also semantically
strictly weaker than SND, and we are explicit about where that matters
(Remark~\ref{rem:semantics}).

When $G$ is random, specifically, when each pair is independently
included in $E$ with probability $p$, Graph-SND is an unbiased
\emph{sampling estimator} of full SND. With Horvitz--Thompson weights,
it has expectation exactly $\SND$, and standard Hoeffding-type
concentration applies after conditioning on the sampled edge count.
This gives an $O(1/\sqrt{m})$ error bound in the number of sampled edges $m$, independent of the number of agents, so $\SND$ can be
approximated to target precision $\varepsilon$ using
$O(\varepsilon^{-2} \log(1/\delta))$ distance evaluations.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A graph-structured generalization $\SNDG$
  (Definition~\ref{def:graph-snd}) that subsumes $\SND$ on the
  complete graph and supports both fixed and random $G$.
\item Two operational interpretations of $\SNDG$ selected by $G$: a
  localized diversity measure (fixed $G$) and a sampling estimator of
  $\SND$ (random $G$ with Horvitz--Thompson weights).
\item Six formal results (Section~\ref{sec:theory}): recovery of
  $\SND$ on $K_n$, non-negativity with zero condition,
  graph-automorphism invariance, a complexity statement counting
  pairwise-distance evaluations, unbiasedness of the Horvitz--Thompson
  estimator, and a Hoeffding-type concentration bound. Proofs appear
  in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.
\item A subgraph-based hierarchical aggregation
  (Section~\ref{sec:method:hierarchical}) that avoids any commitment
  to Wasserstein barycenters in policy space.
\item Experiments on VMAS \citep{bettini2022vmas} verifying (i) that
  Graph-SND on $K_n$ reproduces $\SND$ numerically, (ii) that the sampling estimator concentrates around $\SND$ at the bound predicted by
Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration}, (iii) that sparse Graph-SND
  preserves the measurement and diagnostic uses of $\SND$ at
  substantially reduced cost, and (iv) that Graph-SND matches full
  $\SND$ inside the DiCo closed loop at reduced subroutine cost
  (Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico}).
\end{enumerate}

The construction is deliberately narrow. We do not propose a new
training algorithm, a new policy architecture, or a new
diversity-inducing objective. We propose a drop-in replacement for the
aggregation step of SND that is cheaper to compute at scale and that
retains the properties of SND that matter for how the metric is used
in practice.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Our work sits at the intersection of three lines of research:
diversity measures in multi-agent reinforcement learning,
heterogeneity measures in robotics, and sampling-based approximations
of pairwise statistics. We discuss each in turn and position Graph-SND
against the closest prior work.

\subsection{Diversity measures in multi-agent RL}

Several prior metrics quantify behavioral diversity among learned
policies. \citet{mckee2022diversity} approximate policy distributions
from sampled discrete actions and measure divergence via total
variation; \citet{yu2021informative} similarly rely on empirically
estimated distributions using either KL divergence or Wasserstein on
sampled actions. Both methods introduce sampling error in the
distribution estimation step itself, before any diversity computation.
\citet{hu2022policy} use symmetric KL between discrete action
distributions, which does not satisfy the triangle inequality and is
therefore not a statistical metric. \citet{liu2021unifying} use
$f$-divergence between occupancy measures, yielding a principled
formulation that is computationally intractable beyond zero-sum games.
\citet{parker-holder2020effective} define population diversity as a
determinant of a behavioral distance matrix over deterministic actions
sampled at random states, which can evaluate policies far from their
training support. \citet{lupu2021trajedi} measure Jensen--Shannon
divergence between trajectory distributions for zero-shot coordination
in population-based training.

SND \citep{bettini2025snd} is distinguished from these by being
closed-form on Gaussian continuous-action policies, satisfying the
axioms of a statistical metric, applying to concurrently acting
teams (rather than populations), and providing formal properties that
enable interpretation as behavioral dispersion and measurement of
redundancy. Every prior metric in this list, and SND itself,
aggregates pairwise statistics by averaging uniformly over all
$\binom{n}{2}$ pairs. Our contribution sits at this aggregation step:
Graph-SND generalizes the uniform average to an arbitrary weighted
mean over the edges of a graph, while preserving the closed-form
Wasserstein behavioral distance of SND. All results in this paper
apply directly to the SND setting; they would apply to other
pairwise-distance-based metrics with straightforward modifications.

\subsection{Heterogeneity measures in robotics}

Heterogeneity has a separate history in the robotics literature, where
it often reflects differences in physical capability rather than
policy. \citet{prorok2017impact} formalize robot-swarm heterogeneity
through a species-trait matrix; their analysis targets physical
diversity, which does not always coincide with behavioral diversity
\citep{stanley2019designing}. \citet{li2004learning} propose a
specialization metric obtained via correlation with team performance,
which is an indirect proxy for behavior. \citet{twu2014measure} define
a computation-light metric assuming a fixed number of diversity
classes.

The closest behavioral-space metric is Hierarchic Social Entropy
(HSE) \citep{balch2000hse}, which applies Shannon entropy to a
distribution of agents over hierarchical behavioral clusters. HSE is
deterministic-policy-based and complementary to SND:
\citet{bettini2025snd} show that HSE grows with the number of
equidistant agents and does not capture redundancy, whereas SND is
invariant to the former and decreasing in the latter. Graph-SND
inherits SND's properties on the complete graph and modifies them
predictably on sparser graphs; we leave a parallel study of
HSE-on-a-graph to future work.

\subsection{Graph structure in multi-agent learning}

Graphs are a pervasive modeling device in multi-agent RL, primarily as
communication topologies or message-passing substrates for policy
networks \citep{sukhbaatar2016learning, blumenkamp2021emergence,
bettini2023hetgppo}. They are also used as inductive biases for
credit assignment, role assignment, and attention mechanisms
\citep{wang2020roma, wang2021rode}. Our use of a graph is different:
we do not couple $G$ to the policy or value function. The graph enters
only at the aggregation stage of a diversity measure, as a
specification of which agent pairs contribute to the score.

This decoupling makes $G$ in our formulation compatible with, but
not dependent on, any graph-based policy architecture. When a
communication graph is already available from a GNN-based policy, it
is a natural choice for $G$, and Graph-SND then measures diversity
that is consistent with the interaction structure used by the policy
itself. When no such graph exists, $G$ can be constructed from
spatial or behavioral proximity, or sampled for the purpose of
estimation.

\subsection{Sampling-based approximations and finite-population
concentration}

Our second interpretation, Graph-SND with randomly included edges
as a sampling estimator of $\SND$, draws on standard tools from
survey sampling and concentration inequalities. The Horvitz--Thompson
estimator \citep{horvitz1952generalization} is the textbook device for
unbiased estimation under arbitrary inclusion probabilities, and our
unbiasedness result (Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased}) is a direct
application.

For concentration, we rely on Hoeffding's extension of his inequality
to sampling without replacement from a bounded finite population
\citep[\S 6]{hoeffding1963probability}. Sharper finite-population
bounds are available: \citet{serfling1974probability} introduces a
correction factor that improves the constant when the sampling
fraction is bounded away from zero, and
\citet{bardenet2015concentration} extend this to Bernstein-type and
empirical bounds. We state
Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} in Hoeffding form for clarity and note
these sharpenings in Remark~\ref{rem:serfling}. The sampling-estimator
framing of a sum over all $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs is standard in
$U$-statistics \citep{hoeffding1948class} and has been formalized for
sampling without replacement by \citet{hoeffding1963probability}
and more recently extended to $U$-statistics without replacement in
broader settings. We use only the elementary one-dimensional version;
the link is worth flagging for readers who want sharper rates.

\subsection{Follow-up work on SND}

Since the introduction of SND, \citet{bettini2024dico} have used it as
a control target (DiCo), regularizing training to a prescribed
diversity set-point. \citet{amir2025diversity} study \emph{when}
diversity is beneficial, analyzing curvature properties of cooperative
reward functions. Neither line addresses the scalability of SND
itself. Graph-SND is orthogonal and compositional with both: DiCo can
be instantiated on Graph-SND as a sparse target, and
the conditions for when diversity is rewarded apply independently of
how the diversity metric is aggregated.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Partially Observable Markov Games}
\label{sec:background:pomg}

We consider cooperative multi-agent settings formalized as Partially
Observable Markov Games (POMGs)~\citep{kaelbling1998planning}. A POMG
is a tuple
\[
\bigl\langle \Agents,\; \mathcal{S},\; \{\Obs_i\}_{i \in \Agents},\;
\{\sigma_i\}_{i \in \Agents},\; \{\mathcal{A}_i\}_{i \in \Agents},\;
\{R_i\}_{i \in \Agents},\; \mathcal{T},\; \gamma \bigr\rangle,
\]
where $\Agents = \{1, \dots, n\}$ is the set of agents, $\mathcal{S}$
is the shared state space, and $\Obs_i \subseteq \mathcal{S}$ and
$\mathcal{A}_i$ are the observation and action spaces of agent $i$.
The observation function $\sigma_i : \mathcal{S} \to \Obs_i$ maps
states to agent-$i$ observations, and the reward function
$R_i : \mathcal{S} \times \prod_{j \in \Agents} \mathcal{A}_j \times
\mathcal{S} \to \R$ is shared or individual as the task dictates. The
stochastic transition model $\mathcal{T} : \mathcal{S} \times
\prod_{j \in \Agents} \mathcal{A}_j \times \mathcal{S} \to [0, 1]$
assigns transition probabilities $\mathcal{T}(s^t, \{a_i^t\}_{i \in
\Agents}, s^{t+1})$ given the current state and joint action, and
$\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the discount factor.

Each agent $i$ has a stochastic policy $\policy{i}(a_i \mid o_i)$
parameterized by $\theta_i$ that maps observations to action
distributions. We work in the heterogeneous-policy regime: agents do
not share parameters, so $\theta_i \neq \theta_j$ is permitted for
$i \neq j$. This is the setting in which the question of behavioral
diversity is well-posed, since parameter-sharing constraints force
$d(i, j) = 0$ by construction and collapse the metrics we study to
the trivial value. Agents are trained with any algorithm that supports
per-agent policy parameterization, for example,
(Het)IPPO or (Het)GPPO~\citep{bettini2023hetgppo}, and the metrics
in this paper are computed on the resulting learned policies
\emph{post hoc} on rollout data.

Because we compare behavioral distances between policies, we assume
throughout that all agents share observation and action spaces:
$\Obs_i = \Obs$ and $\mathcal{A}_i = \mathcal{A}$ for all
$i \in \Agents$. This ensures that $\policy{i}(\cdot \mid o)$ and
$\policy{j}(\cdot \mid o)$ are distributions over the same space and
can be compared pointwise in $o$. Extensions to heterogeneous
observation or action spaces require an alignment construction and
are out of scope.

\subsection{System Neural Diversity}
Following \citet{bettini2025snd}, let $\Agents = \{1, \dots, n\}$ be the
agent set and let each agent $i \in \Agents$ have a Gaussian policy
$\policy{i}(o) = \Normal\bigl(\mu_{\theta_i}(o),\, \Sigma_{\theta_i}(o)\bigr)$.
Let $\Rollouts = \{o^t\}_{t=1}^T$ be a collection of joint observation
vectors sampled from policy rollouts, where each
$o^t = (o_1^t,\dots,o_n^t) \in \Obs^n$.
The Monte Carlo behavioral distance between agents $i$ and $j$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dij}
d(i,j) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\Rollouts|\,|\Agents|}
\sum_{o^t \in \Rollouts} \sum_{k \in \Agents}
\Wass\bigl(\policy{i}(o^t_k),\, \policy{j}(o^t_k)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Wass$ admits a closed form on multivariate Gaussians
\citep{bettini2025snd}. The global System Neural Diversity is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:snd}
\SND(\mathbf{D}) \;=\; \binom{n}{2}^{-1} \sum_{i<j} d(i,j),
\end{equation}
the mean of pairwise behavioral distances over unique agent pairs. We work
with~\eqref{eq:dij} directly rather than its integral form, avoiding
specification of a measure on $\Obs$; all our results are stated at the
level of the sampled estimator. Throughout, we treat the values
$\{d(i,j)\}_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs}$ as fixed (deterministic in $\mathbf{D}$),
so that all stochasticity in the estimators below arises solely from the
random graph.

\section{Graph-SND}
\label{sec:method}

Full SND weights every pair equally. This is both computationally
prohibitive (quadratic in $n$) and, in many settings, semantically
imprecise: agents that do not interact or operate on disjoint sub-tasks
contribute to the score on equal footing with coupled pairs. We introduce a
graph-structured generalization that supports two distinct use cases: a
localized diversity measure when $G$ encodes interaction structure, and
sampling-based estimators of $\SND$ when edges of $G$ are drawn from the
pair set.

\subsection{Definition}

Let $\Pairs := \binom{\Agents}{2}$ be the set of unordered agent pairs,
and let $G = (\Agents, E, w)$ be an undirected graph on the agent set with
$E \subseteq \Pairs$ and non-negative edge weights $w : E \to \R_{\geq 0}$.
Write $W(G) = \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} w_{ij}$ and assume $W(G) > 0$. The graph
may be fixed (e.g., communication topology, $k$-NN on observations,
$\varepsilon$-ball) or sampled from pairs
(Section~\ref{sec:method:estimator}).

\begin{definition}[Graph-SND]
\label{def:graph-snd}
The Graph-SND of policies $\{\policy{i}\}_{i \in \Agents}$ on $G$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:graph-snd}
\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) \;=\; \frac{1}{W(G)}
\sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} w_{ij} \, d(i,j),
\end{equation}
with $d(i,j)$ given by~\eqref{eq:dij}.
\end{definition}

When $G = K_n$ with unit weights, $W(G) = \binom{n}{2}$ and
$\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, K_n) = \SND(\mathbf{D})$ identically.

\subsection{Canonical graph families}

Three choices of $G$ recover interpretable special cases.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Complete graph}, $G = K_n$, $w \equiv 1$: recovers $\SND$.
  \item \textbf{$k$-nearest-neighbor graph} in observation space:
  $|E| = O(nk)$, yielding a $\Theta(n/k)$ reduction in the number of
  pairwise distance evaluations relative to full $\SND$.
  \item \textbf{Communication graph} inherited from the policy's message-passing topology: aligns measured diversity with the interaction
  structure actually used by the agents.
\end{itemize}
In each case $G$ may be fixed or state-dependent; the latter requires
re-evaluating $\SNDG$ per rollout batch.

For any weighted graph $G' = (\Agents', E', w')$ with $W(G') = 0$, we define
\[
\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G') := 0
\]
by convention.

\subsection{Hierarchical Graph-SND via subgraphs}
\label{sec:method:hierarchical}

Many multi-agent systems exhibit diversity at multiple scales. We build a
hierarchical construction on scalar diversity values rather than on
policies, avoiding any commitment to policy-space aggregation such as
Wasserstein barycenters.

Let $\{C_1, \dots, C_K\}$ be a partition of $\Agents$ into clusters, and
let $G|_{C_k}$ denote the subgraph of $G$ induced on $C_k$. Define the
within-cluster and between-cluster diversities as
\[
\SNDG^{\mathrm{in}} \;:=\; \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \SNDG\bigl(\mathbf{D}, G|_{C_k}\bigr),
\qquad
\SNDG^{\mathrm{btw}} \;:=\; \SNDG\bigl(\mathbf{D}, G_{\mathrm{cut}}\bigr),
\]
where $G_{\mathrm{cut}}$ contains only the inter-cluster edges of $G$. A
two-level aggregate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hsnd}
\SNDG^h \;=\; \alpha\, \SNDG^{\mathrm{in}} + (1-\alpha)\, \SNDG^{\mathrm{btw}},
\qquad \alpha \in [0,1],
\end{equation}
which generalizes to multiple levels by recursive partitioning.

\section{Theoretical Properties}
\label{sec:theory}

We state Graph-SND's core properties, then give a probabilistic
interpretation as sampling-based estimators of $\SND$ with concentration
guarantees. Proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.

\subsection{Core properties}

\begin{proposition}[Recovery on the complete graph]
\label{prop:recovery}
If $G = K_n$ with unit edge weights, then
$\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) = \SND(\mathbf{D})$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Non-negativity]
\label{prop:nonneg}
$\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $d(i,j) = 0$
for every edge $\{i,j\} \in E$ with $w_{ij} > 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Semantic shift from $\SND$]
\label{rem:semantics}
Proposition~\ref{prop:nonneg} is strictly weaker than the zero condition
of full $\SND$: $\SNDG$ can vanish even when the system contains
behaviorally distinct agents, provided those agents are not adjacent in
$G$. When $G$ encodes task-relevant interaction structure, this is the
intended semantics: Graph-SND measures \emph{locally relevant}
diversity. When full-system diversity is the target, the estimator
interpretation of Section~\ref{sec:method:estimator} is the appropriate
one.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Graph-automorphism invariance]
\label{prop:perm}
For any automorphism $\sigma$ of $(G, w)$,
$\SNDG(\sigma \cdot \mathbf{D}, G) = \SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G)$, where
$(\sigma \cdot \mathbf{D})_{ij} = d(\sigma(i), \sigma(j))$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Complexity]
\label{prop:complexity}
Evaluating $\SNDG$ on a precomputed graph $G$ requires $|E|$ pairwise
distance computations $d(\cdot,\cdot)$, versus $\binom{n}{2}$ for $\SND$.
For $k$-NN graphs with $|E| = O(nk)$, this yields a speedup factor of
$\Theta(n/k)$ in the number of pairwise distance evaluations. This
statement excludes the cost of constructing $G$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Sampling estimators of $\SND$}
\label{sec:method:estimator}

When the goal is to approximate full $\SND$ at reduced cost, we derive
sampling-based estimators from the same graph formalism. Include each pair
$\{i,j\} \in \Pairs$ in $E$ independently with probability $p \in (0, 1]$,
with Horvitz--Thompson weights $w_{ij} = 1/p$ for $\{i,j\} \in E$. Denote
this random graph $G_p$.

\begin{proposition}[Unbiasedness]
\label{prop:unbiased}
The estimator
\[
\widehat{\SND}(G_p) \;:=\; |\Pairs|^{-1}
\sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} w_{ij}\, d(i,j)
\]
satisfies $\E_{G_p}\bigl[\widehat{\SND}(G_p)\bigr] = \SND(\mathbf{D})$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[HT weights make $\SNDG$ a sample mean]
\label{rem:ht-sample-mean}
Under Horvitz--Thompson weights, $W(G_p) = |E(G_p)|/p$, so $\SNDG$ reduces
to the uniform sample mean:
\[
\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G_p)
\;=\; \frac{p}{|E(G_p)|} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} \frac{1}{p}\, d(i,j)
\;=\; \frac{1}{|E(G_p)|} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} d(i,j).
\]
This is the \emph{uniform-weight variant} used in
Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration}; it differs from \mbox{$\widehat{\SND}$} only in normalization.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Concentration]
\label{thm:concentration}
Assume $d(i,j) \in [0, D_{\max}]$ for all $\{i,j\} \in \Pairs$. Let
$m = |E(G_p)|$, and define the uniform-weight variant
\[
\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{D}, G_p) \;:=\; m^{-1}
\sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} d(i,j).
\]
Then for any $\delta \in (0,1)$, conditional on $|E(G_p)| = m \geq 1$,
with probability at least $1 - \delta$ over the randomness of $G_p$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:concentration}
\abs{\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{D}, G_p) - \SND(\mathbf{D})}
\;\leq\; D_{\max} \sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2m}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Sharper finite-population bounds]
\label{rem:serfling}
For sampling fractions $m/|\Pairs|$ bounded away from zero, Serfling's
inequality \citep{serfling1974probability} gives a bound of the form
$D_{\max}\sqrt{(1 - (m-1)/|\Pairs|)\log(2/\delta)/(2m)}$, which is strictly
sharper. See also \citet{bardenet2015concentration} for further
refinements and an empirical-Bernstein variant.
\end{remark}

Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} gives a rate of $O(1/\sqrt{m})$ in the
number of sampled edges, independent of the total number of agents. For
target error $\varepsilon$, it suffices to sample
$m = O\bigl(D_{\max}^2\, \varepsilon^{-2}\, \log(1/\delta)\bigr)$ edges,
which for large $n$ is vastly smaller than $\binom{n}{2}$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We validate the four core claims of Sections~\ref{sec:method}
and~\ref{sec:theory} on a single task: VMAS Multi-Agent Goal Navigation
\citep{bettini2022vmas}, the same didactic scenario used in
\citet{bettini2025snd} to illustrate how SND varies with goal
assignment. The experiment is intentionally narrow: our contribution is
a measurement tool, so the experiments are designed to verify that tool
rather than to demonstrate new training outcomes. All four validations of the core theory share a single code path and
a single set of trained policies; we run them at team sizes $n \in
\{4, 8, 16\}$ on both a random-init checkpoint (iter 0) and a
partially trained checkpoint (iter 100). We then report a 500-iteration
PPO scaling run at $n=100$, and finally a closed-loop integration of
Graph-SND into the DiCo diversity controller \citep{bettini2024dico}
(Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico}).

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:experiments:setup}

\paragraph{Environment and policies.} We use VMAS Multi-Agent Goal
Navigation with continuous actions, \texttt{num\_envs}~$= 32$ parallel
environments, and a rollout horizon of 128 steps. Each agent $i$ has an
independently parameterized Gaussian MLP policy $\policy{i}$ with two
hidden layers of width 64 and per-dimension learnable log\nobreakdash-standard\nobreakdash-deviation, matching the heterogeneous-policy setup of
\citet{bettini2025snd}. Policies are trained with Independent PPO using
the hyperparameters in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}. We do not share
parameters across agents at any point.

\paragraph{Checkpoints.} For each $n \in \{4, 8, 16\}$ we save two
checkpoints. The \emph{iter-0} checkpoint is the random initialization
and serves as a near-homogeneous reference (agents share an
architecture but have distinct random weights). The \emph{iter-100}
checkpoint is after 100 PPO iterations and exhibits substantial
behavioral heterogeneity, with $\SND$ values comparable to those
reported by \citet{bettini2025snd} on the same scenario.

\paragraph{Rollout set.} The Monte Carlo behavioral-distance estimator
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:dij} is evaluated on the rollout observation set
$\Rollouts$ obtained by running the frozen policies for 128 steps
across 32 parallel environments, yielding $|\Rollouts| \cdot |\Agents|
= 128 \cdot 32 \cdot n$ observation-policy pairs. Concretely, this is
16{,}384 evaluations per policy at $n=4$, 32{,}768 at $n=8$, and
65{,}536 at $n=16$.

\paragraph{Metric implementations.} $\SND$ and $\SNDG$ are both
computed directly from the per-observation $(\mu_i, \sigma_i)$ pairs
using the closed-form Gaussian Wasserstein distance
\citep[Definition~1]{bettini2025snd}. For the recovery experiment we
compute $\SNDG$ both by (a) building the full matrix $\mathbf{D}$ and
restricting to edges of $K_n$, and (b) computing pairwise distances
only on the edges of $E$ without materializing $\mathbf{D}$; these two
paths must agree exactly by construction and we verify this as a
sanity check.

\subsection{Recovery on the complete graph (Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery})}
\label{sec:experiments:recovery}

Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery} asserts that $\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, K_n) =
\SND(\mathbf{D})$ exactly. Figure~\ref{fig:recovery} shows both
quantities at $n=4$ on both checkpoints. The absolute error is $0.0$ in
all four cells, matching the proposition up to the numerical precision
at which it can be tested. This is the trivial direction of the
theory, but establishes that the Graph-SND implementation is
correctly aligned with $\SND$ as a point of reference for the
subsequent experiments.

\subsection{Unbiasedness of the Horvitz--Thompson estimator
(Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased})}
\label{sec:experiments:unbiased}

Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased} asserts that
$\E_{G_p}[\widehat{\SND}(G_p)] = \SND(\mathbf{D})$ for any
$p \in (0,1]$. We test this empirically at $n = 8$ by drawing 2{,}000
independent Bernoulli-$p$ graphs for each $p \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5,
0.75\}$, computing $\widehat{\SND}$ on each, and reporting the sample
mean with a 95\% confidence interval.
Figure~\ref{fig:unbiasedness} shows the result. The CIs contain
$\SND(\mathbf{D})$ in every cell across both checkpoints.

As a sharper test, we report the standardized deviation $|\mathrm{bias}|
/ \mathrm{SE}$ for each cell. Under an unbiased estimator with
Gaussian-sampling-distribution error, this quantity is approximately
$\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ in each cell, so we expect most cells to fall below
$|z_{0.975}| = 1.96$ and roughly 5\% of cells to exceed it. Across all
eight $(\text{checkpoint}, p)$ combinations, the maximum observed
$|\mathrm{bias}|/\mathrm{SE}$ is $1.42$, consistent with the
theoretical prediction. The variance of the estimator scales as
$1/p$, visible as the widening CIs at small $p$.

\subsection{Concentration (Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration})}
\label{sec:experiments:concentration}

Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} asserts a Hoeffding-type bound on the
deviation of the uniform-weight variant $\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}$ from
$\SND$. We evaluate this at $n = 16$ (so $|\Pairs| = 120$ unordered
pairs) by drawing 2{,}000 uniform-size edge samples of each size $m
\in \{6, 12, 24, 48, 72, 96\}$ (5\% through 80\% of all pairs),
computing $\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}$ on each, and comparing the empirical
distribution of $|\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}} - \SND|$ against the theoretical
bound at $\delta = 0.10$.

Figure~\ref{fig:concentration} plots three quantities: the Hoeffding
bound $t_H$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration}, the Serfling
finite-population refinement $t_S$ of Remark~\ref{rem:serfling}, and
the empirical 95th-percentile of $|\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}} - \SND|$ over the
2{,}000 draws at each $m$. Across all 12 cells, the empirical
violation rate of the Hoeffding bound is $0.00$: no single draw out of
2{,}000 per cell exceeds $t_H$, well under the allowed $\delta = 0.10$.
The same holds for the Serfling bound.

The empirical line sits well below both theoretical bounds. This is
expected: Hoeffding and Serfling give distribution-free guarantees that
hold for any bounded population, and are therefore worst-case tight
only when the population variance equals $D_{\max}^2/4$. The actual
distribution of $d(i,j)$ on our trained policies has much smaller
variance, so the bounds over-estimate the concentration radius. The
empirical curves still exhibit the predicted $O(1/\sqrt{m})$ slope on
the log-log axis, and the Serfling bound is uniformly tighter than
Hoeffding as predicted, with the gap widening at large sampling
fractions where the finite-population correction matters most.

\subsection{Wall-clock scaling (Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity})}
\label{sec:experiments:timing}

Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity} asserts that $\SNDG$ evaluated on
$|E|$ edges costs $|E|$ pairwise distance computations versus
$\binom{n}{2}$ for $\SND$. To translate this into wall-clock terms, we
time both estimators on the iter-100 checkpoint at $n \in \{4, 8, 16\}$
with Bernoulli inclusion probabilities $p \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75,
1.0\}$ and 20 trials per cell. All timings are on CPU with no other
load.

Figure~\ref{fig:timing} plots the speedup $T_{\mathrm{full}} /
T_{\mathrm{sampled}}$ as a function of $p$, and the absolute wall-clock
times for both paths. At $p = 0.1$ we observe speedups in the range
$10$--$17\times$ across $n \in \{4, 8, 16\}$, decreasing monotonically
to approximately $1\times$ at $p = 1.0$ as expected (since the
Bernoulli graph with $p = 1$ is $K_n$). The speedup at small $p$ is
consistent with the analytical prediction $\binom{n}{2} / \E[|E(G_p)|]
= 1/p$, modulo a small overhead from graph construction and
weight-assignment code paths.

One caveat: these timings measure the cost of the \emph{aggregation
step} of the metric, not the cost of rolling out the policies to
construct the observation set $\Rollouts$. In the regime where rollout
collection dominates total cost (e.g., during training with $\SNDG$ as
a diversity control signal), the wall-clock gains visible here are the
component that scales with $n$; rollout cost is separate and not
targeted by this work.

\subsection{Scaling to $n=100$ under real training dynamics}
\label{sec:experiments:scaling}

The experiments of Sections~\ref{sec:experiments:recovery}
through~\ref{sec:experiments:timing} validate Graph-SND at team sizes
where full $\SND$ remains a tractable baseline. The construction's
motivation, however, is the regime where $\binom{n}{2}$ pairwise
evaluations per measurement become a real bottleneck. We next
demonstrate that the $O(|E|)$ speedup
predicted by Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity} and the concentration
guarantees of Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} hold on a real, extended
training run at $n = 100$.

\paragraph{Setup.} We trained a team of $100$ independently
parameterized Gaussian policies on VMAS Multi-Agent Goal Navigation
using the same Independent PPO configuration as
Section~\ref{sec:experiments:setup}, with VMAS scenario parameters
auto-scaled to accommodate the larger agent count
($\texttt{world\_spawning\_x} = \texttt{world\_spawning\_y} = 4.0$,
$\texttt{agent\_radius} = 0.03$), $\texttt{num\_envs} = 32$, and a
reduced training horizon to fit an overnight run on a single GPU.
Every 25 PPO iterations we logged both $\SND(\mathbf{D})$ (over all
$\binom{100}{2} = 4{,}950$ pairs) and
$\widehat{\SND}(G_{0.1})$ (over one Bernoulli-$0.1$ subsample) on the
current rollout batch, together with the wall-clock cost of each.

\paragraph{Results.} Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-n100} shows both
$\SND(\mathbf{D})$ and $\widehat{\SND}(G_{0.1})$ across 500 PPO
iterations, together with the per-measurement wall-clock speedup.
Three observations stand out.

First, the $\sim\!10\times$ speedup predicted at $p=0.1$ by
Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity} is sustained across the entire
training run, with per-measurement speedup in the range
$9.0\times$--$10.9\times$ and no discernible trend as policies
diversify: the variation is consistent with run-to-run timing
noise around the structural $1/p = 10\times$ predicted ratio.

Second, $\widehat{\SND}(G_{0.1})$ tracks $\SND(\mathbf{D})$
essentially indistinguishably across the run: the two curves overlap
visually at every one of 21 logged iterations, extending the
concentration guarantee of Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} from the
frozen-checkpoint regime of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments:concentration} to the non-stationary
setting of an actual training run.

Third, the SND trajectory itself, rising from a near-homogeneous
initialization to a peak of $\approx 0.185$ around iter 50--100,
then consolidating to a steady state of $\approx 0.14$ by iter 500, reflects realistic training dynamics: policies initially
diversify to solve the navigation task individually, then
coordinate toward a more compact joint solution as training
progresses. The point is not the specific trajectory but that
Graph-SND tracks it correctly.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Together with the frozen-checkpoint
concentration experiment of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments:concentration}, this extends the
empirical case for Graph-SND into the regime it was designed for. At
$n = 100$, computing the full $\SND$ at every PPO update costs
$\approx\!1.2$ seconds of aggregation alone per measurement; the
sampled estimator reduces this to $\approx\!0.12$ seconds while
preserving the measurement to within noise. For any downstream use
that queries diversity inside a training loop --- for example, DiCo
\citep{bettini2024dico} --- this is the difference between a
negligible overhead and a measurable fraction of update time.

\subsection{Downstream diversity control}
\label{sec:experiments:dico}

To demonstrate downstream utility beyond frozen-policy measurement, we
integrate Graph-SND into the DiCo heterogeneity controller of
\citet{bettini2024dico}. DiCo regularizes training toward a target
diversity $\SND_{\mathrm{des}}$ using the full $O(n^2)$ aggregator; we
replace only that aggregator with Graph-SND on VMAS Multi-Agent Goal
Navigation at $n = 16$, holding $\SND_{\mathrm{des}} = 0.1$ and comparing
full pairwise $\SND$ ($K_n$) to uniform-weight Bernoulli subgraphs at
$p \in \{0.25, 0.10\}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:dico-pilot} summarizes the run.
Mean reward and \emph{applied} SND---the post-scaling diversity the
controller actually regulates toward $\SND_{\mathrm{des}}$---track
almost identically across all three estimators: Graph-SND preserves
DiCo's closed-loop behavior while cutting the median CUDA-synchronized
wall-clock time of the diversity subroutine per training batch.
Because Graph-SND evaluates the same pairwise Gaussian kernel on each
sampled edge, it is a high-fidelity, drop-in replacement at the metric
API for diversity control under the same set point.

\subsection{Summary and limitations}
\label{sec:experiments:summary}

The controlled benchmark experiments, together with the downstream DiCo
integration of Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico}, jointly validate the
theory section. Recovery is
exact; HT unbiasedness holds to within the expected sampling noise;
the Hoeffding and Serfling concentration bounds are respected in
every cell with room to spare; the wall-clock behavior tracks the
$\binom{n}{2}/|E|$ speedup ratio of Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity};
and the scaling experiment at $n=100$ demonstrates that this speedup
and the tracking fidelity of $\widehat{\SND}$ survive 500 iterations
of active PPO training.

We note three scope limits of these experiments. First, our core property validations range only up to $n=16$, with scaling demonstrated at $n=100$. This is large enough to make $\SND$'s quadratic cost visible but small enough that the quadratic cost remains tractable as a baseline. Validating Graph-SND at $n \gg 100$ is the natural next experiment but requires training infrastructure beyond what we use here. Second, we test only one scenario (Multi-Agent Goal Navigation).
The mathematical content of the theorems is scenario-independent, so
we expect the results to transfer, but a multi-scenario study is
future work. Third, we do not yet test the localized-measure
interpretation of $\SNDG$ on a task where the graph $G$ encodes a
meaningful interaction structure; that is the natural follow-up
experiment and we defer it to a separate paper.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Graph-SND is deliberately narrow. We have not proposed a new training
algorithm, policy architecture, or diversity-inducing objective; we
have replaced the aggregation step of $\SND$ with a construction that
is parameterized by a graph $G$, recovers $\SND$ exactly when $G$ is
complete, and scales linearly in $|E|$ instead of quadratically in
$n$. Everything downstream of the metric, how heterogeneity is
induced, how policies are parameterized, how diversity is controlled, is unchanged. The virtue of this narrowness is that the results
compose cleanly with existing work: any method that consumes $\SND$ as
a scalar signal can consume $\SNDG$ instead, with the choice of $G$
determining what that signal means.

The two interpretations of $\SNDG$ are a feature of the formalism
rather than two separate tools. A fixed $G$ expresses a modeling
commitment, that only certain agent pairs are relevant to the
diversity question being asked, and the metric answers accordingly.
A random $G$ with Horvitz--Thompson weights expresses an estimation
commitment: that $\SND$ itself is the object of interest, and
computational cost, not interaction structure, is the reason to use a
sparse graph. Having both interpretations fall out of the same
definition means a practitioner can switch between them without
re-implementing anything, and the theoretical guarantees are
non-overlapping: fixed-$G$ results give exact recovery and invariance,
random-$G$ results give unbiasedness and concentration.

The most immediate downstream application is behavioral diversity
control. \citet{bettini2024dico} use $\SND$ as a reference signal
during training (DiCo), regularizing the training loss toward a
target diversity value $\SND_{\mathrm{des}}$. Because DiCo treats the
metric as a black-box scalar, $\SNDG$ can be substituted directly:
the control objective becomes $\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) =
\SND_{\mathrm{des}}$, with $G$ chosen according to the use case. A
fixed $G$ converts DiCo into local diversity control, which may be
desirable in tasks with spatial or functional locality (e.g.,
controlling diversity within squads but not between squads in a
large cooperative game). A random $G$ converts DiCo into a
compute-subsampled variant of standard diversity control, where the
per-update cost of evaluating the control target drops from
$O(n^2)$ to $O(m)$ with $m$ tunable by the user. We expect the random-$G$ variant to be the more straightforward mechanical
substitution; the fixed-$G$ variant raises design questions about how $G$
should be chosen and whether it should be static or learned jointly with
the policy. Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico} already shows that the
random-$G$ substitution composes cleanly in DiCo at one representative
setting; broader sweeps over tasks, algorithms, and controller
hyperparameters remain open.

\subsection*{Limitations}

We flag five scope limits beyond those noted in the experimental summary (Section~\ref{sec:experiments:summary}). First, our core experiments range only up to $n = 16$, with a single scaling demonstration at $n=100$. This lower range is where full $\SND$ remains a tractable baseline, which is necessary for the recovery and unbiasedness validations, but validating $\SNDG$ at $n \gg 100$ is the natural next experiment; the limiting factor is MARL training infrastructure at scale, not the metric itself.

Second, we test on a single scenario. Multi-Agent Goal Navigation is
the scenario in which $\SND$ was originally validated by
\citet{bettini2025snd}, so it is a natural starting point, but the
full empirical case for $\SNDG$ requires showing that the recovery,
unbiasedness, and concentration results hold across the dynamic tasks
(Flocking in Wind) and diversity-control tasks (Differential
Steering) from the original paper. We expect them to, because the
results are scenario-independent at the mathematical level, but an
empirical multi-scenario study is future work.

Third, we do not test the localized-measure interpretation of
$\SNDG$ on a task where $G$ carries task-relevant structure. The
recovery and sampling experiments use either $K_n$ or randomly
sampled subgraphs, both of which treat $\SNDG$ as an approximation of
$\SND$ rather than as a separate measure. The fixed-$G$
interpretation's usefulness is a claim about semantics, not about
computation, and testing it requires a task where local diversity and
global diversity mean different things. This is a standalone
empirical paper and we defer it.

Fourth, all timings reported in Section~\ref{sec:experiments:timing}
are CPU-only. On GPU, the cost profile changes materially: full
$\SND$ parallelizes well across pairs, and the $\binom{n}{2}/|E|$
speedup ratio of Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity} may shrink when
the full pairwise computation fits comfortably in a single GPU kernel. At $n = 16$, the full pair set is only 120 evaluations, well within the capacity of a single kernel; the regime where GPU parallelism saturates likely begins around $n \geq 50$--$100$. We expect $\SNDG$ to retain a wall-clock advantage when $n$
is large enough that the full pair set exceeds GPU memory, or when
the metric must be evaluated inside a training loop at every PPO
update, but a careful GPU study is deferred.

Fifth, Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico} reports one DiCo configuration
(Independent PPO, one set point, one task). We do not claim optimality,
stability across hyperparameters, or task-by-task robustness of the
closed loop; exhaustive DiCo$+$Graph-SND studies remain future work.

\subsection*{Future work}

Beyond resolving each of the limitations above, four extensions are
natural. The first is learned or task-adaptive $G$: the graph is
currently a user input, but in many applications the interaction
structure is itself something the system should discover (e.g., via
attention weights in a message-passing policy). Treating $G$ as a
learnable object, with $\SNDG$ computed on the current estimate, connects the metric directly to graph-structured MARL policies.

The second is extension beyond Gaussian policies. The closed-form
Wasserstein distance that makes $\SND$ practical relies on Gaussian
action distributions; policies outside this family (categorical
actions, normalizing flows, mixture policies) require either
closed-form Wasserstein for the chosen family or a principled
approximation. We conjecture that the graph-structured aggregation of
$\SNDG$ is orthogonal to the choice of behavioral distance: the
propositions and Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} go through for any
non-negative behavioral distance bounded in $[0, D_{\max}]$, with
only the computational characterization
(Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity}) depending on the per-pair cost of
the distance itself. Verifying this in practice, e.g., on discrete-action tasks with a total-variation or empirically-estimated Wasserstein, is a direct next experiment.

The third is a deterministic distortion bound for the fixed-$G$
interpretation. The sampling-based concentration result
(Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration}) gives a guarantee on the
random-$G$ estimator but says nothing about how a fixed, structured
$G$ relates to $\SND$. A useful bound would take the form
$|\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) - \SND(\mathbf{D})| \leq f(G, \mathbf{D})$
under explicit conditions on the regularity of $\mathbf{D}$ over $G$
(e.g., a Lipschitz condition on the policy in an observation
embedding, with $G$ chosen consistent with that embedding). We
explored this direction in an earlier version of the paper and
concluded that the cleanest such result requires a careful coupling
between the behavioral distance and the graph that is worth treating
as a separate theoretical contribution. We leave it as open.

The fourth is extending the DiCo integration of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico} into a full controlled study: larger
$n$, additional tasks and algorithms, sweeps over $\SND_{\mathrm{des}}$
and $p$, and analysis of how Bernoulli sampling noise in
$\widehat{\SND}(G_p)$ interacts with set-point regulation in DiCo
\citep{bettini2024dico}.
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced Graph-SND, a graph-structured generalization of System
Neural Diversity that replaces SND's uniform average over all
$\binom{n}{2}$ agent pairs with a normalized weighted mean over the
edges of a user-specified graph $G$. The construction admits two
interpretations selected by the choice of $G$: a localized diversity
measure when $G$ encodes task-relevant interaction structure, and an
unbiased sampling estimator of full SND when edges are drawn
independently with Horvitz--Thompson weights. We proved six formal
results, recovery on $K_n$, non-negativity, graph-automorphism
invariance, a complexity reduction, unbiasedness, and Hoeffding-type
concentration, and verified all of them experimentally on VMAS Multi-Agent Goal Navigation at team sizes $n \in \{4, 8, 16, 100\}$.

The experiments confirm that recovery is numerically exact, the
Horvitz--Thompson estimator is unbiased to within expected sampling
noise, the concentration bound holds in every cell with room to spare
(zero violations across 24{,}000 draws versus an allowed rate of
$\delta = 0.1$), and wall-clock speedup tracks the analytical $\binom{n}{2}/|E|$ ratio at each inclusion probability $p$, and sustains a ${\sim}10\times$ speedup across 500 PPO iterations at $n=100$ while the sampling estimator tracks full \SND{} throughout.
Section~\ref{sec:experiments:dico} shows the same substitution inside
DiCo at $n=16$: reward and applied SND match the full metric while
subroutine time drops.

Graph-SND is a drop-in replacement for the aggregation step of SND.
Any method that consumes SND as a scalar signal, including the DiCo
diversity-control framework, can substitute Graph-SND immediately,
with the choice of $G$ determining whether the signal measures local
diversity or approximates global diversity at reduced cost. All
experimental code is available at \url{https://github.com/codingwithshawnyt/Graph-SND}.

Two directions are natural next steps. Validation at $n \geq 500$ or in distributed training regimes would further exercise the regime where the $O(n^2) \to O(|E|)$ speedup matters most in practice. A deterministic distortion bound for the
fixed-$G$ interpretation, complementing the probabilistic
Hoeffding guarantee of Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration}, would
characterize how closely Graph-SND approximates full SND when $G$ is
specified by task structure rather than sampled. Beyond these, the
question of how to \emph{learn} or task-adapt $G$ during training
deserves its own study.

\section{Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

We use the conventions of Section~\ref{sec:background}: $\Agents$ is the
agent set with $|\Agents| = n$; $\Pairs = \binom{\Agents}{2}$ is the set
of unordered agent pairs, with $|\Pairs| = \binom{n}{2}$; $d(i,j) \geq 0$
is the Monte Carlo behavioral distance~\eqref{eq:dij}, treated as a
deterministic scalar given the rollout set $\Rollouts$; and $G = (\Agents,
E, w)$ has non-negative edge weights $w_{ij}$ with total weight
$W(G) = \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} w_{ij} > 0$.

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery} (Recovery)}

If $G = K_n$ with $w_{ij} = 1$ for all $\{i,j\} \in \Pairs$, then
$E = \Pairs$ and $W(G) = |\Pairs| = \binom{n}{2}$. Substituting
into~\eqref{eq:graph-snd},
\[
\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, K_n)
\;=\; \frac{1}{\binom{n}{2}} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs} 1 \cdot d(i,j)
\;=\; \binom{n}{2}^{-1} \sum_{i < j} d(i,j)
\;=\; \SND(\mathbf{D}). \qed
\]

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:nonneg} (Non-negativity)}

Each term $w_{ij} d(i,j)$ in~\eqref{eq:graph-snd} is a product of
non-negative factors, so the numerator $\sum_{E} w_{ij} d(i,j) \geq 0$.
Since $W(G) > 0$ by assumption, $\SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G) \geq 0$.

For the equality condition, $\SNDG = 0$ iff
$\sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} w_{ij} d(i,j) = 0$. A sum of non-negative terms is
zero iff each term is zero, so the equality holds iff $w_{ij} d(i,j) = 0$
for every $\{i,j\} \in E$. Equivalently, $d(i,j) = 0$ for every edge
$\{i,j\} \in E$ with $w_{ij} > 0$. \qed

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:perm} (Automorphism invariance)}

Let $\sigma : \Agents \to \Agents$ be an automorphism of $(G, w)$, meaning
$\sigma$ is a bijection satisfying $\{i,j\} \in E \iff \{\sigma(i),
\sigma(j)\} \in E$ and $w_{ij} = w_{\sigma(i)\sigma(j)}$ for all
$\{i,j\} \in E$. Then
\begin{align*}
\SNDG(\sigma \cdot \mathbf{D}, G)
&\;=\; \frac{1}{W(G)} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} w_{ij}\, d(\sigma(i), \sigma(j)).
\end{align*}
Reindex the sum by $\{i', j'\} = \{\sigma(i), \sigma(j)\}$. Because
$\sigma$ is an automorphism, the map $\{i,j\} \mapsto \{\sigma(i),
\sigma(j)\}$ is a bijection $E \to E$, and $w_{ij} =
w_{\sigma(i)\sigma(j)} = w_{i'j'}$. Hence
\[
\SNDG(\sigma \cdot \mathbf{D}, G)
\;=\; \frac{1}{W(G)} \sum_{\{i',j'\} \in E} w_{i'j'}\, d(i', j')
\;=\; \SNDG(\mathbf{D}, G). \qed
\]

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:complexity} (Complexity)}

Evaluating~\eqref{eq:graph-snd} on a precomputed graph $G$ reduces to
computing $d(i,j)$ for each $\{i,j\} \in E$ and forming a weighted sum. The
sum costs $O(|E|)$ floating-point operations, dominated by the $|E|$
pairwise-distance computations. $\SND$ in~\eqref{eq:snd} instead requires
$|\Pairs| = \binom{n}{2}$ such computations. Since each pairwise-distance
computation has the same cost in both cases, the speedup factor in the
number of pairwise-distance evaluations is $\binom{n}{2}/|E|$. For $k$-NN
graphs $|E| = O(nk)$, giving a factor of $\Theta(n/k)$. This statement
does not include the cost of constructing $G$. \qed

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased} (Unbiasedness)}

For each $\{i,j\} \in \Pairs$, let $Z_{ij} = \ind[\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)]$.
By construction, $\{Z_{ij}\}_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs}$ are independent
Bernoulli$(p)$ random variables. With $w_{ij} = 1/p$ on edges of $G_p$,
\[
\widehat{\SND}(G_p)
\;=\; |\Pairs|^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} w_{ij}\, d(i,j)
\;=\; |\Pairs|^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs} Z_{ij} \cdot \tfrac{1}{p}
\cdot d(i,j).
\]
Taking expectation and using $\E[Z_{ij}] = p$,
\[
\E_{G_p}\!\left[\widehat{\SND}(G_p)\right]
\;=\; |\Pairs|^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs}
p \cdot \tfrac{1}{p} \cdot d(i,j)
\;=\; |\Pairs|^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs} d(i,j)
\;=\; \SND(\mathbf{D}). \qed
\]

\subsection*{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:concentration} (Concentration)}

We first establish that, conditional on $|E(G_p)| = m$, $E(G_p)$ is a
uniform random subset of $\Pairs$ of size $m$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:conditional-uniform}
Let $\{Z_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \Pairs}$ be i.i.d.\ Bernoulli$(p)$ with
$p \in (0,1)$, and let $E = \{\alpha : Z_\alpha = 1\}$. Conditional on
$|E| = m$ with $0 \leq m \leq |\Pairs|$, the set $E$ is uniformly
distributed over subsets of $\Pairs$ of size $m$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
For any fixed $T \subseteq \Pairs$ with $|T| = m$,
\[
\Prob(E = T \mid |E| = m)
\;=\; \frac{\Prob(E = T)}{\Prob(|E| = m)}
\;=\; \frac{p^m (1-p)^{|\Pairs|-m}}
          {\binom{|\Pairs|}{m} p^m (1-p)^{|\Pairs|-m}}
\;=\; \binom{|\Pairs|}{m}^{-1},
\]
which does not depend on $T$.
\end{proof}

Now fix $m \geq 1$ and condition on $|E(G_p)| = m$. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:conditional-uniform}, $E(G_p)$ is a uniform random sample
of size $m$ without replacement from the finite population
\[
\Pi \;:=\; \bigl\{ d(i,j) : \{i,j\} \in \Pairs \bigr\},
\]
where $|\Pi| = |\Pairs|$ and each $d(i,j) \in [0, D_{\max}]$. The
population mean is
\[
\mu \;:=\; |\Pairs|^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in \Pairs} d(i,j) \;=\; \SND(\mathbf{D}),
\]
and the sample mean is precisely
\[
\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{D}, G_p) \;=\; m^{-1} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E(G_p)} d(i,j).
\]

\citet[Section 6, Theorem 4]{hoeffding1963probability} establishes that
the two-sided Hoeffding inequality applies to sampling without
replacement from a bounded finite population: for any $t > 0$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hoeff-wor}
\Prob\bigl(\abs{\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}} - \SND} \geq t \;\bigm|\; |E(G_p)| = m\bigr)
\;\leq\; 2\exp\!\left(- \frac{2 m t^2}{D_{\max}^2}\right).
\end{equation}
Setting the right-hand side equal to $\delta$ and solving for $t$ gives
\[
t \;=\; D_{\max}\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2m}}.
\]
Thus with probability at least $1 - \delta$ conditional on $|E(G_p)| = m$,
\[
\abs{\SNDG^{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{D}, G_p) - \SND(\mathbf{D})}
\;\leq\; D_{\max}\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2m}}. \qed
\]
